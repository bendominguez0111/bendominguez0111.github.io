Writeup · 01 · GamblyBot

100 requests a day to 100 a minute, in 48 hours.

how the first image-to-betslip bot outgrew its mvp before we finished celebrating it

The MVP

We built the first version of GamblyBot in December 2024: two models and a loop. An X model watched our mentions, and a betslip creator turned what it found into something you could actually bet. Tag the bot under any screenshot of a bet, and it would parse the legs, match them against live odds, and reply with a one-tap slip. We released it on X a week before the Super Bowl.

The architecture was the simplest thing that could work: a polling loop. Every cycle it pulled recent mentions, walked them one at a time (download the image, run classification, match offers, post the reply) and went back to sleep. Serial processing, no queue, no cache. Between the polling window and the per-request latency, the ceiling worked out to roughly a hundred requests a day.

Which was fine. We had maybe twenty.

The spike

There was no launch announcement. We soft-launched by calling the bot ourselves under sports-betting posts. People saw a reply appear with a one-tap slip and tried it on their own posts; their followers saw those replies and did the same. The growth was exponential in the most literal sense — every reply was an ad for the next one. Within two days, traffic went from a hundred requests a day to a hundred requests a minute, and the mentions feed became a firehose of strangers tagging us under every slip on the timeline. The math stopped working immediately: demand of a hundred requests a minute against an API tier that allowed a hundred replies a day. The bot sold out of its reply budget minutes after reset. A thousand users a week became a hundred thousand overnight.

Behind the rate cap, every weakness of the loop surfaced at once. The polling window overflowed, so mentions were silently missed. Serial processing meant each reply queued behind every reply before it — latency went from seconds to hours. Retries double-posted. Rate limits locked us out mid-cycle, which made the backlog worse, which made the next cycle longer. The bot wasn't down, exactly. It was answering Tuesday's bets on Thursday.

The scramble

The fix was the boring, correct one: put a queue between ingestion and work (and upgrade our API tier). Mentions landed in a queue the moment we saw them; a pool of workers pulled them off concurrently. Classification and reply delivery split into separate stages so a slow model call couldn't block a finished reply. Dedupe keyed on the post, not the attempt, killed the double-posting. Later, a perceptual-hash cache short-circuited the screenshots everyone kept re-sending — the same viral slip, tagged a hundred times, became one classification and ninety-nine cache hits. The rearchitected pipeline held: over the span of the X chapter, deeplink clicks went from a few thousand to ten million, and the bot sent millions of betslips across platforms.

The end of the X chapter

The X bot didn't die of load — we'd solved that. In September 2025, X cut off both us and Pikkit, the competitor whose launch had pushed us to double down. A media company had negotiated an exclusive deal for the category; we never got a proposal or a chance to bid. That's platform dependency in its purest form: build your front door on someone else's API, and their business development becomes your architecture.

Discord sustained us. The bettors were already there — group servers where picks get shared every night — and a socket-based bot in a server you own has no polling window, no per-mention economics, and no platform risk you didn't sign up for. Discord became the durable channel, and the pattern it proved — meet bettors inside their group chats — drove everything after: iMessage in 2025, Telegram in 2026, and the share-sheet flows in the app.

What I'd tell you

  • the mvp's job is to find demand. ours found it in 48 hours
  • put the queue in before the spike. ingest and work are different jobs
  • cache the modality that repeats — viral screenshots repeat a lot
  • never build your front door on someone else's api — it can be sold out from under you
  • the durable channel is the one where your users already talk to each other
